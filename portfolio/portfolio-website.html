Problem

Having a well structured portfolio that puts the user first is an important differentiator when trying to secure a position in design. Creating engaging case studies that balance respect for readers time while also showcasing the depth of the work I am able to produce is a difficult challenge.

Goal

Create an engaging, memorable portfolio that stands out to hiring managers. This portfolio must easy to understand and must clearly communicate my abilities as a designer.

Action

I've spent over 5 years building out my portfolio website, constantly iterating and improving based on feedback. The current version is v3.0 and was launched in December of 2020. I handle the research, design, code and deployment of the site.

Impact

I've secured multiple full time and freelance positions using this website. You can track the change history for the site on Github. I've been trusted to lead projects and teams as a result of my previous work and the way it is communicated on this site. This website is frequently used by professors and advisors of Laurier's UX program (and co-op stream) as an example of an effective portfolio website.

Problem space

Context

When I first created this website, I was unsure of what my future held in the design world. I had been experimenting with graphic and print media design in high school and was unsure of where I would do my post-secondary education. Frankly, I had little desire to be a fine artist, but I wanted to pursue a discipline where I could combine my passion for creating engaging designs and my ever-present contemplation of how things work and why they work the way they do. Eventually I found a post-secondary degree program that I was quite happy with and here I am, on track to graduate from Wilfrid Laurier University with an Honors Bachelor of Design degree.

There came a time before even entering into that degree program though where I felt it was important to showcase my work online. Many university programs I was considering required portfolios to be considered for entry, and creating an online portfolio that could be used for multiple applications felt like a logical and efficient way to keep my options open. The purpose for the site as it current stands is different in some ways than the purpose for which it was originally built, but in many ways the core function remains the same.

Nowadays, a portfolio is a requirement for many job postings in this space. Specifically, recruiters often look to an applicant's portfolio to understand whether that designer can do the work, and do it well. Good portfolios document process in a way that showcases an applicant's talent, and good applicants are able to tell a captivating story with a limited amount of time and attention from a recruiter. It's an interesting requirement, but one that provides ample opportunity when carefully considered. The reason you're seeing this case study is because I like to think I've done just that with my portfolio website.

Goal

The site is built with the goal of effectively communicating my ability to do good work with and for the right people. This core mission of the site has, in essence, remained the same since I first built it. Throughout many iterations and opportunities to sit on the hiring side of the table, I have come to understand more about the users and what they're looking for.

Understanding the user

Figure 1.1: A user story

Figure 1.2: An empathy map

When I first built the site, I knew very little about what it took to get hired in the industry. I hadn't gone through the hiring process, and I hadn't hired any designers. The industry wasn't as evolved as it is now, and Medium wasn't crowded with posts detailing how to stand out like it is today. The original version of the site reflected my general lack of understanding of what my users really needed.

The core of the mission of the site, as mentioned, is to communicate my value as a designer to the hiring manager. I knew I needed to take a closer look at that user group and try and better understand their true aims. I re-designed and re-launched the site in December 2020 with improvements based on my new experiences and understanding of common hiring practices.

What do users want to see?

I remember vividly the moment where the user needs really clicked for me. In conversation with an industry professional responsible for hiring designers at their company they said (and I apologize for the poor paraphrasing):

"As a hiring manager, I want you to succeed. I want to see your portfolio and be able to tell right away that you have the potential to be right for the job. Make it easy for me to see what you're capable of."

Over the years, a number of key insights have stuck out from conversations like these. Here are some highlights of what I've come to understand:

  • Users are not as passionate about your projects as you are
  • Users are willing to give you only a quick glance by default, in hopes of seeing something that encourages them to read more
  • Using images and graphics to communicate your process is much more effective than words

Understanding what makes up an effective website

The two central cornerstones of this sites value are simplicity and discoverability. We've established that users want my work to be easy to understand, and to be easy to understand my work has to be easy to find. This has prompted a couple of key changes over the years.

Figure 1.7: The intentionally simple header navigation

Figure 1.8: The descriptive title format, with breadcrumbs describing site navigation

I made sure to keep the site's core page structure intentionally simple.

  • Home, About, Portfolio, Blog and Connect make up the only navigation options listed in the header and the footer
  • Each page title clearly serves a distinct purpose and one should not reasonably be confused for another
  • I provide users with breadcrumbs wherever possible to inform them of where they are on the site

As I began to build out more and more work, it became harder to find work of any specific type when visiting /portfolio. By providing hiring managers the option to sort content, users should be able to find exactly what they're looking for.

Figure 1.9: Sorting options found on the /portfolio page

The sorting options were originally grouped by medium (eg ux, graphic, print design) and have since been updated to be sorted by which company the work was done as a part of. This heirarchy for sorting projects also helps to establish my credibility as someone who has served multiple internships and worked with multiple high-profile companies.

Figure 1.10: Transitioning my domain and personal brand

When I launched v3.0 of this portfolio website, I transitioned the domain and branding from idkjoel.co to joelschellenberger.com. In the spirit of being more discoverable, transitioning away from short-hand, informal branding to branding that is easy to locate based on my full name was important. Also, idk is short form for "I don't know" and originally this was meant to reflect my willingness to learn, but over time I've grown less comfortable having my first introduction to folks being the phrase "I don't know."

Creating scalable, easy-to-build content structures

One of the large-scale changes I made with v3.0 of the website was to re-visit the way I structured case studies. Specifically, when hoping to make pages easier to understand, I transitioned to the format you're currently reading:

Figure 1.11: The standard page format across case studies

This structure was chosen for a couple reasons: first of all, it breaks down the core structure of each article in something that is easier to skim or scan for someone with limited time. It's simple, and can be summarized easily at the top of each page so that a hiring manager can quickly evaluate whether or not they're interested in continuing to read about that project.

Having consistent structure across case studies also helps to keep the content generation process easier on my end: previously, writing case studies was a tedious task. Having a blank page with infinite potential layouts can be beautiful, but can also impede progress. By establishing a standard format, I'm able to quickly generate key talking points (often the sub-headings used throughout each section of the write-up) and expand as needed.

My philosophy minor can get in the way when it comes to writing content. I'm passionate about ethics, rhetoric, and argumentation. Philosophical arguments are often written in less-than-simple English. Frankly, argumentation benefits when concepts are broken down into their most simple forms. The hope is that I am able to break things down simply on this page and on this site.

The site is also built to be mobile responsive. Feedback from users has been overwhelmingly consistent when it comes to how users prefer to view portfolios (spoiler alert: it's on desktop), but there are a non-zero amount of users who view on mobile. By leaning on established web design best practices and leveraging Bootstrap, I'm able to build a mobile-responsive site on an established grid without too much trouble. Having a framework for building pages that makes it easy and quick means I spend less time working on the architecture of the portfolio and more time refining the content.

Using images to help make points

It might feel like it goes without saying, so I won't spend long on it: images help readers understand your content when done right. They are not a "silver bullet" or a replacement for well-written content, but they can definitely help users understand what you're trying to say. They also work to keep your users attention. From what I've heard in feedback sessions, users often skim pages looking for images that catch their eye and then read around that image for explanations.

Figure 1.12: An image, to help communicate the point

I have come to take the approach of "show, don't tell" when it comes to site content. Because communicating process is so important, pictures are worth a ton. However, it can be equally ineffective to post a lot of photos without adequate explanations. I've tilted toward the content-heavy side of the spectrum throughout the history of the site, and am actively adding more and more images with each new revision. I think hybrid images are especially effective: images that blend simple, easy to understand text content with visual interest such as colours or icons.

Simpler, faster, better content design

I'll be blunt: I am not a trained content designer. I have experience designing content, and have been asked to design content for previous work. I am far from an expert, and far from perfect when it comes to generating high impact, low friction content. I have so much respect for people who do this for a living.

Something I picked up from my super-talented co-workers at Ontario Digital Service is the "simpler, faster, better" mantra. This phrase is the core of government service in Ontario: we want to build with these three key goals in mind. The content folks are a key part of that: government services are complex, difficult to understand webs of interconnected patchwork. It's up to the very talented content design folks to make it all make sense, and help the teams that build services work toward making their services make sense (without the content folks holding their hands).

When I'm writing content for this site, I work toward simple content. Content that is easy to read, easy to understand, easy to make sense of is the goal. This is obviously easier said than done, and I am always learning new best practices. I really do try and simplify the language I use on the site, and this benefits all visitors. It's meant to make the site simple and fast, and ultimately easier to understand. I try to reduce the reading level for content wherever possible.

Building an accessible, inclusive site that works for everyone

I make intentional efforts to support as wide a net of users as possible. Once again, I would like to re-iterate that I am not perfect when it comes to accessible design. I do my best to support users, and be inclusive of all cultures where possible. I work hard to include as wide a net of users as possible when conducting testing, and make intentional efforts to reach Black, Indigenous and People of Colour. I also actively use diverse illustrations whenever possible.

I'm working to add proper alt-tags and image descriptions to all images on the site. Because I didn't really understand what these resources did for people who use assistive devices in the early development of this site, I neglected to add these. This is something I am actively working on.

I also include an Indigenous land acknowledgment on every case study page. This is something that I feel is important: I want to acknowledge the land I am present on. I'm grateful for the opportunities to learn, grow, play and build things on this land, and by making the intentional acknowledgment, I hope to encourage others to consider how they can support Indigenous communities. The whole discipline of human-centered design is about having empathy for each other, and it would feel wrong not to be empathetic to the issues facing others.

A point of feedback heard from hiring managers is that a portfolio should reflect some personality: because "fit" is such an important factor in hiring nowadays, I want to show my personality on this site in the hopes of connecting with someone who is hiring. I like to think that I'm a considerate person who is inclusive of my peers and willing to constantly learn and grow, and I like to think that comes out on this site.

Being inclusive, respecting diversity, acknowledging privilege... these should all be more than just talking points: they should be actions actively taken in my work. It is important to me to bring these things with me when building products and services meant to help people. I aspire to be the type of person who is actively considerate of others, no matter the background.

Reflection

Any time that I spend working on this portfolio is time I'm investing in my own growth. I have put hundreds of hours into this website, coding everything and learning along the way what goes into a good website. It's not perfect, but it is the sum of many years of work.

When considering what I would change about this site, there are a few things that come to mind. With more time and money available I would have loved to have done more formal usability testing with compensation for users. Obviously that is out of reach for now, but something I plan to do in the future. With even more time, I'd like to take a finer comb to the site in hopes of refining the content design and really tightening up the descriptions. Alas, the nature of the work is iterative and as I've cautioned myself over the years, done is better than perfect. Fail fast, fail often.
