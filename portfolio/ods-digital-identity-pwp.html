Problem

There was no framework / strategy that enabled people of Ontario to utilize secure, online identity verification supported by government documentation. There was also no way to digitally represent physical documentation provided by the government prior to this project.

Goal

The development of a robust framework and accompanying digital touchpoint that empowers people of Ontario to manage their digital identity (as identity access / management develops from it's current standing).

Action

I was on a small team responsible for research and development related to the proposed model and standards for Ontario's digital identity solution. I personally worked to solidify the proposed user-centric model for digital identity management and developed a series of thorough user flows, app mockups, and more to communicate and validate the foundational work.

Impact

The proposed digital identity solution will launch to the people of Ontario in 2021/2022.

Problem Space

Context

Digital identity and identity access management each represent very complex fields. While I will be attempting to summarize it for the sake of accessible storytelling on this page, I encourage you to read more on the subject if it interests you.
During my 12-month work term with the Ontario Digital Service, I was fortunate to work on a project with very large implications: I was placed on a team tasked with establishing a digital identity solution for the people of Ontario. Digital identity, is a large and complex field with many intricacies; my role on this team consisted of being an advocate for human-centered design while the province worked to build out this project from a technical standpoint. That role took many different forms; I worked with one other UX designer to develop a functional and accessible mental model for our implementation and represent that model using user flows, UI mockups, user research and ultimately sharing results with the larger development team responsible for technical implementation and business / policy implementations.

Digital identity is more than a digital copy of your physical ID; while this is a key feature of many digital identity frameworks, digital identity as a concept is much larger than that specific instance.
Digital identity, put simply, is a means to verify that a person is who they say they are online, so that they are able to verify their right to access sensitive information.
The potential implementations and use cases of digital identity solutions span far and wide; potential use cases include accessing sensitive online government services such as medical information with confidence in the security of the service, or presenting government-certified credentials to institutions such as banks or vendors who are required to verify a customers age.

These are just two examples of the thousands of potential important use cases that digital identity can work to be a solution for. The implementation of digital identity solutions for citizens is currently being handled on a case-by-case basis in Canada and internationally, but the sentiment surrounding the necessity of some form of digital identity is overwhelmingly consistent: governments will need to provide some form of digital identity and secure online identity solutions for their citizens in the next 5-10 years.

Enter: Ontario's digital identity team!

Research (Potential Models for Digital Identity Implementations)

I joined the digital identity team in May 2020; I will be summarizing research in this section that occurred before I joined the team, which was done by other members of the team. Do keep in mind that I will not be attempting to take credit for this work; rather, I will be summarizing their findings as a means to tell an effective story and to help frame the work that I was in fact responsible for once I joined the team.
The landscape of "identity" as a concept has evolved greatly over the years, and there are a few potential models that have been adopted by humans as technology has advanced and identity solutions have come to mass adoption:

  • Centralized Identity (administrative control by a single authority or hierarchy)
  • Federated Identity (administrative control by multiple, federated authorities)
  • User-Centric Identity (individual or administrative control across multiple authorities without requiring a federation)
  • Self-Sovereign Identity (individual control across any number of authorities)

For more reading on the different types of identity in these contexts, I recommend reading this blog post.

The model Ontario is moving forward with is referred to as a verifiable-credential model. This model can be classified as a Self-Sovereign Identity (or SSI) model and this model is a decentralized implementation that prioritizes user privacy and independence. Rather than a user having a unique identity for every product and service that requires you to have a verified identity, you are able to utilize your singular, unique digital identity to access those services.
From a technical perspective, the verifiable credential model allows a government ministry (or an approved third-party vendor) to issue a digital credential, which has a series of data points attached to it. Users can consent to share data from their verified digital credentials in one-time, verified transactions.
The team of developers and architects working on this project over the years have come to the conclusion that this model will be the dominant model on the global identity scale moving forward, and it was important to us as designers to adhere to this model to ensure the prioritization of user privacy, user security, and ease of access.
It's also worth mentioning that part of the responsibility held by the team was to work with the other provinces and territories of Canada to develop the Pan-Canadian Trust Framework, a project meant to encourage the inter-operability of each areas digital identity solution so that Canadians are able to utilize their provincial digital identity solutions no matter where in the country they are attempting to use them.

Other Key Findings from Research Phase

As mentioned previously, we inherited the work of an experience architect (and outsourced design firm) who mocked-up an initial user-flow for setting up a digital ID wallet. I'll show it below, but there were a number of flaws that were uncovered as the team's collective understanding of stakeholder needs evolved. The flow was not particularly fleshed-out, and truthfully the team found much more value in a detailed literature review and "competitive" analysis phase that allowed us to develop an understanding of the pillars that make up effective identity solutions.

Here are some of the key findings from our research uncovered:

  • It is essential for adoption and public support that government take a privacy-focused approach.
  • The development of trust in digital government is a cornerstone of mass adoption.
  • By utilizing the verifiable credential model, we will be able to build a foundational product that can serve as a building block for future innovations and security enhancements in this space.
