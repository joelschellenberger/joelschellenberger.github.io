Problem

Chat support is a common feature across the web today. As a province, we needed to address the growing need for web-based support in a post-pandemic world.

Goal

Our goal was to develop a robust chat support solution that could scale effectively. It was important to match the current understanding of how online chat support looks and feels for users. We were also required to adhere to the current design system and standards of Ontario.ca.

Action

I served as a design and research lead on this project. I facilitated feedback sessions, create personas, created prototypes, tested those prototypes and more. I also took on a teaching role with my stakeholders so that they could continue to focus on human-centred design after I left the project.

Impact

I wrote thorough developer handoff documentation, which is being used to develop the beta. The beta is launching across 44 different program areas. This research will inform the launch of online chat support across Ontario.ca, reaching millions of users.

Problem space

Context

Chat support is a feature that users have come to expect from websites on today's internet. The opportunities to provide helpful support to users (and often to automate this support) are something that the Ontario Public Service wants to be involved with. As such, I was tasked with working on a team of technical and business stakeholders to research and design an effective chat solution that could meet the needs of visitors on Ontario.ca.

Although discovery had previously been done as a part of the vendor procurement process, there was little in the way of human-centred design work that had been done prior to my engagement with the project. Throughout the project, I not only completed this work to a high standard but I was also encouraged by my colleages to take on a teaching role so that the business and technical stakeholders could continue the practice after I was moved to a new project.
It's also worth mentioning that this work was done during the COVID-19 pandemic and as such we were limited in our testing potential to folks who could connect with us digitally. Typically, we would hope to conduct tests in person with users who are not required to have an internet connection.

Goal

How might we deliver a user-friendly chat interface to the people of Ontario that meets and exceeds user expectations for features, wait times, and overall satisfaction with the service? Additionally: how might we gather measurable feedback throughout the design and delivery of this service to continually iterate and improve?

Research objectives

  • What features and appearance do users expect from online chat support?
  • What sort of preferences do users have when seeking support?
  • What will the button to start a chat look like, and where will it appear?
  • What will the button to start a chat look like, and where will it appear?

Key considerations

  • Dealing with some pre-defined vendor behaviors
  • Some users have pre-defined mental models for how chat functions
  • The chat interface needs to align closely with the design system
